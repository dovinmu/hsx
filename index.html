There's no business like predicting the financial details of show business¶

Rowan Copley (March 2016)

Lately I've been trying to predict how much money a film will make in the box office. I started playing around with the idea because I play the Hollywood Stock Exchange, trading fake Internet dollars for "stocks" in a film to try to predict how much it will sell in tickets. It's a fun way of keeping up with which movies are coming out and how good I think they'll be. And of course, as any real-life stock trader will tell you, having data models can really help you beat the market.

So my model basically exploits this fact, and just copies the first week over and over again, shrinking it down by some amount.

If the model doesn't have the full first week, it tries to estimate the other days. The way it estimates the other days is it basically assumes that a weekend take will be five times a weekday's. Kind of hand-wavy, kinda based on the data, but it's good enough for our purposes. The model keeps pace for the first few days, but then steadily falls behind. Meanwhile, it's all over the place for Star Wars, but manages to get a lot more accurate as more data is added.

In [18]:

Star Wars: The Force Awakens had an amazing (and record-shattering) opening day, which throws the model quite off-track, expecting the film to keep soaring and gross 900 million domestically in the first four weeks. But there are only so many people who go to the theater, and a huge number went on opening night. After the weekend, the model swings wildly in the other direction, predicting a domestic gross of only 700 million. Once we look at the whole week the model is doing pretty well, coming within 4% of predicting the eventual outcome.

The films at the very extremes are the outliers, and we shouldn't really expect our model to predict them. Rock the Kasbah was by all accounts a terrible film, scoring 10% on Rotten Tomatoes, while Suffragette was a limited-release picture that didn't hit its stride until its third week in theaters.

Let's cut out the bottom and top 10% and then look at the extremes of the middle 80%.

In [28]:
idx = int(len(df) / 10)
lower = min(matrix[idx:-idx])
upper = max(matrix[idx:-idx])
print('{0}% to {1}%'.format(lower, upper))

-28.0% to 24.0%

So if we get the first seven days of a film's box office records and our 7-day model estimates that it will gross 100 million total in the first four weeks, then we can say there's an 80% chance it will gross no more than:

In [29]:
lower = lower/100
upper = upper/100
estimate_m = 100
print('Estimate: {0}m, 80% confidence: {1}m, {2}m'.format(estimate_m, estimate_m - estimate_m * abs(lower), estimate_m + estimate_m * upper))

Estimate: 100m, 80% confidence: 72.0m, 124.0m

Not very amazing, though it's a start. It's a pretty simple model. It's not paying attention to reviews, it's barely even paying attention to whether even during the first week ticket sales are ballooning or crashing to the ground. But we can do better.

There is a correlation between the model's accuracy and the amount that the film ended up grossing. If we look only at films that grossed over 50 million, then a lot of the biggest extremes in over- or underperforming are left out.

In [30]:
over50m = df[df['gross'] >= 50000000]
matrix = over50m['cumError'].sort_values()
idx = int(len(df) / 10)
lower = min(matrix[idx:-idx])
upper = max(matrix[idx:-idx])
print('{0}% to {1}%'.format(lower, upper))
lower = lower/100
upper = upper/100
estimate_m = 100
print('Estimate: {0}m, 80% confidence: {1}m, {2}m'.format(estimate_m, estimate_m - estimate_m * abs(lower), estimate_m + estimate_m * upper))

-5.0% to 11.0%
Estimate: 100m, 80% confidence: 95.0m, 111.0m

Now that's a bit better! Without even looking at anything but the first week of ticket sales we've got a reasonably confident model that doesn't give uselessly wide predictions.

With more data from years other than 2015 I'll feel more confident about computing the relationship between opening week gross and confidence interval for the eventual cumulative total of sales. Until then, thanks for reading!
